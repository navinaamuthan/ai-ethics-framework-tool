## Intended Purpose
The proposed installation of camera-based emotion recognition systems in secondary school classrooms poses significant risks to children's rights, privacy, and dignity. The system's potential for biased emotion recognition, lack of transparency, and inadequate consent mechanisms may lead to psychological harm, discrimination, and surveillance. Furthermore, the system's impact on students' emotional well-being and the potential for misuse of collected data are concerning. The risks are exacerbated by the system's primary validation on adult Western faces, which may not accurately represent the diverse student population.

## Risk Category Breakdown
### Accountability
- Lack of transparency (Medium): The system's lack of transparency regarding its decision-making processes and data collection methods may lead to a lack of trust among students and parents.
### ChildrenRights
- Inadequate consent mechanisms (High): The system's reliance on parental consent without providing students with an individual opt-out mechanism may violate their rights to autonomy and privacy.
### DataGovernance
- Data governance (Medium): The system's data collection and storage methods may pose risks to data security and governance, potentially leading to data breaches or misuse.
### Dignity
- Dignity (High): The system's objectification of students' emotions may undermine their dignity and autonomy.
### Discrimination
- Biased emotion recognition (High): The system's potential for biased emotion recognition may lead to unfair treatment of students based on their ethnicity, gender, or other protected characteristics.
### PrivacyBreach
- Privacy breach (High): The system's collection and storage of students' emotional data may pose a significant risk to their privacy and confidentiality.
### PsychologicalHarm
- Psychological harm (Medium): The system's potential to cause emotional distress or manipulation may lead to psychological harm among students.
### Surveillance
- Surveillance (High): The system's constant monitoring of students' emotions may create a pervasive surveillance environment, potentially infringing on their right to privacy.
